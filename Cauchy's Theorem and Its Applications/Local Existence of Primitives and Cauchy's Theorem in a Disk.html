Local Existence of Primitives and Cauchy’s Theorem in a Disk

3.2. Local Existence of Primitives and Cauchy’s Theorem in a Disk#

Theorem 3.2

If \(f\) is holomorphic in an open disk \(D \subseteq \C\), then \(f\) has a primitive there.

Proof. Without loss of generality, we may assume the disk \(D\) is centered at the origin. If it is centered at \(z_0\), we can translate the disk to the origin and define \(f(z) = f(z + z_0)\). Then we find a primitive for \(f\), say \(F\). The primitive for \(f\) is then given by \(F(z) = F(z - z_0)\).

Assume \(D\) is centered at the origin, we are going to construct a primitive \(F\) for \(f\) in \(D\) using a contour integral. For any point \(z \in D\), curve \(\gamma_z\) is taken to be the horizontal line segment from \(0\) to \(\RE z\) followed by the vertical segment from \(\RE z\) to \(z\). Note that \(\gamma_z\) is indeed contained in \(D\). Let function \(F\) be defined by

\[F(z) = \int_{\gamma_z} f(w) \dif w \]

We will show \(F\) is indeed a primitive for \(f\) in \(D\). Hence, we need to estimate the quotient \(\frac{F(z+h) - F(z)}{h}\) when \(h \to 0\).

This invites us to consider the contour integral of \(f\) along curve \(\gamma_{z_h} - \gamma_z\). As illustrated in Fig. 3.1, by adding a rectangle and a triangle without change the value of the contour integral due to Theorem 3.1 and Corollary 3.1, one may verify that

(3.1)#\[F(z+h) - F(z) = \int_{\gamma_{z+h} - \gamma_z} f(w) \dif w = \int_\eta f(w) \dif w\]

where \(\eta\) is the line segment from \(z\) to \(z+h\).

Now, we are going to estimate \(f(w)\) about the point \(z\). Because \(f\) is continuous, we can write

\[f(w) = f(z) + \psi(w), \quad w \in D \setminus\{z\}\]

where \(\psi(w)\) is continuous and

\[\lim_{w \to z}\psi(w) = 0 \]

Note

Estimating \(f(w)\) about \(z\) using the derivative of \(f\) at \(z\), like what we did in the proof of Theorem 3.1, may be an overkill. It suffices to estimate \(f(w)\) by exploiting the continuity of \(f\). We have already used the fact that \(f\) is holomorphic to construct the curve \(\eta\) by applying the Goursat’s theorem.

It then follows that

(3.2)#\[\begin{split}\int_\eta f(w) \dif w & = \int_\eta f(z) + \psi(w) \dif w \nonumber\\& = f(z) \int_\eta 1 \dif w + \int_\eta\psi(w) \dif w \nonumber\\& = f(z) h + \int_\eta\psi(w) \dif w\end{split}\]

Combining (3.1) and (3.2), we have

(3.3)#\[\begin{split}\abs{\frac{F(z+h) - F(z)}{h} - f(z)}& = \frac{1}{\abs{h}}\abs{\int_\eta \psi(w) \dif w}\nonumber\\& \text{Apply the ML inequality}\nonumber\\& \leq\frac{1}{\abs{h}}\max_{w \in \eta}\abs{\psi(w)}\abs{h}\nonumber\\& = \max_{w \in \eta}\abs{\psi(w)}\end{split}\]

As \(h \to 0\), we have \(w \to z\) and hence \(\psi(w) \to 0\). Therefore, the limit of the right-hand side of (3.3) is \(0\) as \(h \to 0\). This proves \(F^\prime(z) = f(z)\).
